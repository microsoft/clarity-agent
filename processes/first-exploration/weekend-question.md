# Scenario: The Weekend Question

## Seed situation

A small neighborhood café has been open seven days a week for three years. The owner is burning out and considering closing on weekends — which happen to be the busiest days. Regular customers, employees, and the local community all have a stake in what happens next.

### Intentional ambiguities

- Whether the problem is the weekend schedule specifically, or something structural about how the business runs.
- Whether burnout is caused by hours, decision fatigue, financial stress, isolation, or something else.
- Whether "the café" and "the owner" are separable — could the business exist without the owner present?
- What "success" means — personal sustainability, revenue growth, community service, or some combination.
- Whether the weekend customers and weekday customers are the same people, overlapping, or distinct.

## Dimensions and tensions

- **Stated problem vs. root cause.** The owner says "I need weekends off." But the exhaustion might come from something the schedule change won't fix — financial anxiety, decision load, lack of help, or identity entanglement with the business.
- **Hidden cross-subsidies.** Revenue streams that appear independent may be coupled. Weekend activity might generate weekday loyalty in ways that aren't visible in daily sales numbers.
- **Optimization vs. resilience.** Closing weekends optimizes for the owner's immediate need but may reduce the system's ability to absorb future shocks (revenue loss, staff turnover, competitor entry).
- **Identity and separability.** Is the café's value inseparable from the owner's presence? If so, any solution that removes the owner changes what the café is.
- **Stakeholder asymmetry.** The owner bears the burnout cost; customers and employees bear the schedule-change cost. Neither group fully sees the other's experience.
- **Reversibility.** Closing weekends is nominally reversible (just reopen). But lost habits, customers, and staff hours may not return easily.

## Deepening questions

- "What would change if the problem were something other than the schedule?" (probes root cause)
- "Are the weekend customers also weekday customers, or different people?" (probes hidden coupling)
- "If you close weekends and revenue drops, at what point does it become a worse problem than burnout?" (surfaces irreversibility threshold)
- "Could the café be the café without the owner present on a given day?" (probes identity/separability)
- "Who is absorbing the cost of the current situation that you haven't named yet?" (surfaces invisible stakeholders)
- "What would you need to believe to try this without existential risk?" (identifies preconditions)

## Indicators of promising insight

- The user proposes "just hire someone" — probe what trust, training, and identity transfer that requires.
- The user focuses on revenue — probe whether the real fear is financial ruin or loss of the thing they built.
- The user treats weekend closure as obviously reversible — probe what habits and relationships are hard to rebuild.
- The user separates "the business" from "the owner" easily — probe whether customers and staff see it the same way.
- The user identifies employees as stakeholders — probe whether reduced hours help them (predictability) or hurt them (income).

## Premise changes for reassessment

- A loyal regular offers to invest as a silent partner, providing six months of financial runway to experiment. *(Reduces immediate financial risk but introduces obligation and time pressure.)*
- A nearby competitor opens and is open on weekends. *(Makes the closure decision harder to reverse — the customers now have somewhere else to go.)*
- A key employee offers to manage weekends independently. *(Tests the separability question directly — but introduces delegation risk.)*
- The owner realizes the real exhaustion source is supplier logistics on Monday mornings, not weekend service. *(Root cause reattribution — the proposed solution no longer addresses the actual problem.)*

## Fallback examples

*Use these only when the conversation stalls. They are illustrative, not destinations.*

**Example insight (not the insight):** Weekend brunch customers also come on Tuesday mornings. The weekend experience is what built the loyalty — remove it and the weekday traffic declines too. The owner sees weekends as the problem but they're actually the foundation.

**Example requirement (not the requirement):** "The business must operate in a way that the owner can sustain for years — without destroying the customer relationships that make it viable."

**Example failure chain (not the failure):** Owner closes weekends → weekday revenue drops over 8 weeks → investigation reveals weekend customers were also the weekday regulars → reopening weekends doesn't restore the lost habits → the closure created a worse financial crisis than the burnout it was meant to solve.

## Anti-pattern

Do not sample several dimensions aloud or present a menu of risks after the user responds. Choose one promising thread and deepen it. Serendipity should feel like discovery, not coverage.
