# Scenario: Renovate or Move

## Seed situation

A family has outgrown their current home. They're debating whether to renovate (add space, reconfigure layout) or sell and move to a larger place in a different neighborhood. Both options are expensive, disruptive, and hard to reverse.

### Intentional ambiguities

- Whether "more space" is the actual need, or a proxy for something else (privacy, separation from work, a life-stage transition).
- Whether the family's composition in five years will resemble its current composition.
- Whether the neighborhood — with its invisible daily-life infrastructure — is part of the decision or background.
- What counts as "enough" and who defines it.
- Whether the constraints are financial, spatial, relational, or temporal — or some blend.

## Dimensions and tensions

- **Snapshot optimization.** Any design (renovation plan or house search) encodes assumptions about who the family is right now. Families change — sometimes predictably, sometimes not.
- **Invisible infrastructure.** Daily life depends on an ecosystem that's hard to see until it's gone: the neighbor who watches the kids, the walking-distance school, the familiar grocery store, the commute route. These don't appear on a pro/con list but are load-bearing.
- **Stated need vs. underlying requirement.** "We need another bedroom" might really mean "we need acoustic separation" or "someone needs a space that is theirs." The solution depends on which requirement is real.
- **Irreversibility asymmetry.** Moving is more irreversible than it appears (community, school continuity, relationships). Renovating may be less irreversible but creates a long period of disruption.
- **Misaligned incentives.** Contractors, real estate agents, and lenders all benefit from the decision going forward — but not necessarily from the right decision for the family.
- **Scope creep and commitment escalation.** Renovations expand once walls are opened. Moves expand once you start browsing listings. Both have a ratchet effect.

## Deepening questions

- "If space isn't the real problem, what is?" (probes stated vs. underlying need)
- "What parts of your daily life would you lose if you moved?" (surfaces invisible infrastructure)
- "Who in the family has a different answer to 'what do we need'?" (probes stakeholder divergence)
- "What's your picture of this household in five years?" (surfaces temporal assumptions)
- "If the renovation takes twice as long as planned, what breaks?" (probes disruption tolerance)
- "What would have to be true about the new neighborhood for moving to actually work?" (identifies unstated preconditions)

## Indicators of promising insight

- The user lists bedrooms as the need — probe whether it's really about privacy, noise, or identity (a teenager's need is different from a toddler's).
- The user treats "stay" and "go" as the only options — probe hybrid approaches or the underlying requirement that both are trying to satisfy.
- The user focuses on the house — probe what happens to the daily-life ecosystem (school, community, routines).
- The user assumes the family won't change — probe what's predictable (kid ages out, parent ages in) and what's not.
- The user mentions a home office — probe whether the work-from-home assumption is stable or could shift.
- The user frames it as purely financial — probe what money can't buy back (community, continuity, the kids' sense of home).

## Premise changes for reassessment

- One parent's employer announces full return-to-office in three months. *(The home office that drove the space need is no longer needed. What else changes?)*
- An aging parent asks whether they could move in within the year. *(Adds a new person to the household equation — accessibility, ground-floor needs, relationship dynamics.)*
- The family discovers their neighborhood is being rezoned for higher density, increasing property values but changing the area's character. *(The "stay" option now means staying in a changing place, not the same place.)*
- A close friend on the same street announces they're moving away. *(Tests whether the invisible infrastructure was already eroding — the community factor may have a shorter shelf life than assumed.)*

## Fallback examples

*Use these only when the conversation stalls. They are illustrative, not destinations.*

**Example insight (not the insight):** The family renovates for their current needs — office, playroom, extra bedroom. Three years later, the kids are teenagers who need different space, the parent commutes again, and a grandparent needs ground-floor accessibility. The renovation served the family-as-it-was, not the family-as-it-is.

**Example requirement (not the requirement):** "The family needs a living arrangement that accommodates evolving needs without sacrificing the relationships and routines that make their current life work."

**Example failure chain (not the failure):** Family moves for more space → within a year, they realize the old neighborhood's walking-distance school and trusted neighbor were load-bearing daily-life infrastructure → the new house has more rooms but less livability → the problem they actually had (daily logistics under strain) is now worse.

## Anti-pattern

Do not sample several dimensions aloud or present a menu of risks after the user responds. Choose one promising thread and deepen it. Serendipity should feel like discovery, not coverage.
